Why cosine similarity ignores pure magnitude: Cosine similarity measures the cosine of the angle between two vectors, which depends solely on their direction and not their length. When vectors are normalized (scaled to unit length), their magnitudes become 1, so the denominator ||q|| × ||d|| equals 1, and the cosine reduces to just the dot product of the unit vectors. This means that two documents with identical term distributions but different total word counts (i.e., one is simply a scaled version of the other) will receive the same cosine score, because scaling a vector changes its magnitude but not its direction.

Part 5 — Scaling Thought Experiment (3 pts)

Question: If every component of d4 were doubled, would cos(q, d4) change?
Let d4′ = 2 · d4 = [2, 4, 2, 0, 0]

New dot product:   q · d4′ = 2 × (q · d4) = 2 × 5 = 10

New norm:   ||d4′|| = 2 × ||d4|| = 2√6

New cosine:
cos(q, d4′) = 10 / (√6 × 2√6) = 10 / (2 × 6) = 10 / 12 = 5/6 ≈ 0.833
Answer: No, cos(q, d4) would not change. Doubling every component of d4 multiplies the dot product by 2 (numerator becomes 2 × original) and also multiplies the norm ||d4|| by 2 (denominator becomes 2 × original). These factors of 2 cancel exactly in the fraction, leaving the cosine value unchanged at 5/6 ≈ 0.833. More generally, for any positive scalar c, cos(q, c · d) = (c · q · d) / (||q|| × c · ||d||) = cos(q, d), confirming that cosine similarity is invariant to uniform scaling of either vector.
